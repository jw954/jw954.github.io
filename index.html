Junhao Wang (Harry)

hry.wang[at]mail.utoronto.ca

I'm a 4th year student in Engineering Science at the University of Toronto, majoring in Machine Intelligence. My research interests lie at the intersection of computational imaging, computer vision, and multimodal machine learning. I'm passionate about building practical systems that push the boundaries of what's possible with visual data, particularly in extreme conditions where conventional imaging fails.

Currently, I am working on my undergraduate thesis at the Toronto Computational Imaging Group, focusing on generating color images and videos from binary quanta frames using single-photon imaging at 100,000 fps. Previously, I've contributed to surgical scene reconstruction with 4D Gaussian Splatting at CMU's Safe AI Lab, developed pixel-coded epipolar imaging systems, and worked on ML compiler engineering for Cerebras Systems' Wafer-Scale Engine.

My work spans computational imaging, 3D/4D reconstruction, differentiable rendering, and safe/robust vision systems. I'm interested in PhD programs in Computer Vision, Machine Learning, or related Engineering fields where I can continue developing innovative imaging and vision systems.

Selected Publications

FeatureEndo-4DGS: Real-Time Deformable Surgical Scene Reconstruction and Segmentation with 4D Gaussian Splatting

Kai Li*, Junhao Wang*, William Han, Ding Zhao — ML4H Symposium 2025 (PMLR) / arXiv preprint

Introduces the first real-time 4D Gaussian Splatting pipeline that jointly reconstructs and segments deformable surgical scenes via feature distillation from 2D foundation models. Outperformed SOTA methods on PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, and RMSE metrics.

23,000-Exposures/s 360fps-Readout Software-Defined Image Sensor with Motion-Adaptive Spatially Varying Imaging Speed

Roberto Rangel, Xiaonong Sun, Ayandev Barman, Rahul Gulve, Savo Bajic, Jingmin Wang, Harry Wang, David B. Lindell, Kiriakos N. Kutulakos, Roman Genov — IEEE VLSI 2024 / paper

Developed automated fundamental matrix calculation and RAFT-inspired optical flow methods for pixel-coded epipolar imaging, enabling motion-adaptive spatially varying imaging at extreme frame rates.
